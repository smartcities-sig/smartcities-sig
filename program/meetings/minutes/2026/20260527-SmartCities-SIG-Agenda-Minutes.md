# Meeting Summary

This meeting marked the formal kickoff of the Smart Cities Interoperability Initiative, a collaborative effort involving municipalities, standards organizations, academia, and industry participants. The initiative is intended to deliver practical interoperability outputs and demonstrations in preparation for the Smart City Expo World Congress 2026 in Barcelona.

The meeting focused on:

- Introducing participating organizations and motivations
- Presenting the proposed governance and collaboration model
- Reviewing the repository structure and tooling
- Explaining the proposed interoperability methodology
- Discussing participation agreements and contribution rules
- Reviewing the initial use cases submitted by Madrid Digital Office
- Establishing meeting cadence and next steps

The group emphasized that this initiative is not an OMA standards group, but rather a collaborative SIG-style activity designed to coordinate interoperability analysis and reusable semantic models across organizations.

---

# Discussion Topics

## 1. Initiative Objectives and Vision

### Summary

Joaquin Prado explained that the initiative aims to create a practical interoperability collaboration environment focused on municipal operational pain points and reusable semantic interoperability models.

The initiative will operate as a proof-of-concept effort between May and November 2026, culminating in outputs and demonstrations for Smart City Expo Barcelona.

The core objectives discussed included:

- Capturing municipal operational pain points
- Extracting reusable interoperability abstractions
- Identifying gaps in existing standards ecosystems
- Coordinating standards organizations around shared semantic models
- Producing reusable Smart Data Models consumable by Digital Twins

Juan Muñoz emphasized that municipalities are seeking convergence of data models rather than creation of additional incompatible models. He explained that municipalities need practical mechanisms to translate operational knowledge into sustainable digital models.

Participants highlighted interest in:

- Digital twins
- Semantic interoperability
- Shared schemas and reusable abstractions
- Machine-readable interoperability models
- Avoiding reinvention across standards organizations

Matt Gilmore emphasized the importance of semantic understanding and machine-readable interoperability rather than focusing on specific serialization formats such as JSON or XML.

---

## 2. Governance and Working Model

### Summary

Joaquin Prado presented the proposed governance and operational framework for the initiative.

Key points discussed included:

- The initiative operates independently from OMA standards processes
- A dedicated GitHub organization and repository structure has already been created
- The repository currently contains draft governance, methodology, and process documents
- Work will follow an asynchronous collaboration model
- Agreements are only considered approved during formal meetings
- Draft work will occur in the `dev` branch
- Approved content will move into the `main` branch

The group discussed the need for:

- Transparency
- Consensus-based decision making
- Impartial program management
- Lightweight governance
- Clear distinction between draft and approved content

Juan Muñoz recommended explicitly documenting the meaning of the `main` and `dev` branches to simplify onboarding for external participants.

Joaquin Prado confirmed that all current repository content should be considered draft material subject to review and pull requests. The branch to work is the default branch `dev`.

---

## 3. Participation Agreement and IP Protection

### Summary

A significant portion of the meeting focused on the proposed participation agreement and intellectual property protections.

Joaquin Prado explained that:

- The SIG itself is not a legal entity
- Participating organizations need lightweight protections against IP contamination
- Contributions must be public and non-confidential
- Contributions are intended to be reusable under a CC BY 4.0 style model
- Standards organizations must remain protected from externally encumbered intellectual property

Juanjo Hierro raised questions regarding attribution obligations under the proposed CC BY licensing approach.

Seth Newberry clarified that attribution would apply to the Smart Cities SIG initiative as the source organization rather than requiring contributor registration at the individual content level.

The group agreed that:

- The participation agreement is the highest immediate priority
- Organizations should review the agreement internally
- The agreement must remain lightweight and practical
- Formal onboarding and repository write access ultimately depend on signature completion

Joaquin Prado stated that comments and pull requests against the participation agreement are encouraged before approval.

## 5. Proposed Interoperability Methodology

### Summary

Joaquin Prado presented the first draft of the proposed interoperability methodology.

The methodology was described as a multi-stage process:

### Stage 1 — Municipal Operational Reality

Municipalities provide:

- Operational pain points
- Use cases
- Measurement requirements
- Contextual constraints
- Service objectives

### Stage 2 — Reusable Interoperability Abstractions

The SIG extracts reusable semantic concepts and interoperability abstractions from municipal use cases.

Examples discussed included:

- Service outcome measurements
- Device output measurements
- Energy consumption
- Measurement scope
- Location context
- Operational context

### Stage 3 — Standards and Smart Data Models Integration

Standards organizations analyze:

- Existing standards support
- Missing components
- Required semantic mappings
- Reusable model structures

Outputs are expected to align with Smart Data Models and Digital Twin consumption requirements.

The methodology discussion generated extensive conversation around:

- Semantic interoperability
- Smart Data Models
- Smart Objects
- Schemas
- Machine readability
- Context representation

Alberto Bella clarified that the proper terminology is “Smart Data Models” rather than “Smart Data Objects.”

Matt Gilmore stressed the importance of shared semantic understanding regardless of serialization technology.

Juanjo Hierro suggested clarifying abstraction levels and relationship to Smart Data Models entity/property structures.

The group agreed that the methodology remains draft material and requires further refinement through pull requests and review.

## 6. Initial Use Cases

### Summary

The meeting reviewed the initial municipal use cases submitted by Madrid Digital Office.

Two primary use cases were identified:

1. Public Street Lighting
2. Water Metering / Water Consumption

Joaquin Prado explained that the Public Street Lighting use case had already undergone an initial semantic analysis to demonstrate the methodology.

The analysis explored:

- Lux vs lumens vs watts distinctions
- Operational context
- Measurement scope
- Service outcome semantics
- Device measurements
- Infrastructure measurements

Juan Muñoz emphasized that the submitted documents are not final technical specifications but operational starting points that will require input from municipal domain experts.

Discussion also clarified that:

- The initiative should remain focused and avoid excessive scope expansion
- Additional use cases may be considered later
- Public lighting and water monitoring are sufficient initial domains for the current timeline

Juanjo Hierro asked about previously discussed tree/falling-tree scenarios, and Juan Muñoz explained that those use cases were intentionally deferred to keep the scope manageable.
